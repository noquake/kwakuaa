    James Baldwin

    Go Tell It on the Mountain (1953)

    This was a really heavy book to read. Much like Giovanni's Room, Baldwin has this way of showing depth in minor interactions but in this one we get full expositions in the history of the core cast and those most important to them. The past loves of John's father, the tragedy of his mother. Despite being ancient history or altogether nonexistent for John, they actively shape how he grows up today. And much like the black story in general, it always seems to seep into everything else in their lives from how they decide to love each other and themselves. The book takes on a whole new level of gravity when we realize its a semi-autobiographical work and it starts to make sense as we understand what Baldwin draws from, how he creates his other works. Baldwin is addictive and with this one I feel like we're starting to wade into such deep water that even after finishing the book the weight of it all lingers.

    Giovanni's Room (1956)

    This book is perfect. It only ended all too soon, this may be my new all time favorite. Baldwin's writing is everything I've been looking for and so, so much more. His prose is so elegant and seemingly effortless. His dialogue always says just enough while the internal descriptions of characters and their actual actions reveal a whole new dimension of complexity. This book felt so real. I can't say how much of it I relate to personally but this layer of human-ness is so thick and permeable that it's impossible to not feel something in reading it. Giovanni's room is an amazing piece.
