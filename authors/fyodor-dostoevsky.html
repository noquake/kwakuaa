    Fyodor Dostoevsky

    Crime and Punishment (1866)

    This is one of those books that reads like it must have been fun to write.

    The whole point of this book is that crime in and of itself begets punishment. Punishment, in a sane and regular mind, is needed, or at the very least innately desired. And without it or some kind of external reconciliation from society or others, the mind inflicts it upon itself. Rodya said something very similar to that in the beginning of the novel but focused on how that extraordinary person would feel no such repercussions because it would be their duty to commit those crimes and better society as a whole.

    This implies that it is only via unsavory means and by extraordinary people that society can as a whole progress. Rodya in his youth and confidence does not believe that these rules apply to him and chooses to rid society of an old lady known to swindle and deal, but unfortunately kills her sister as well.

    The book then follows Rodya's long and twisted downfall through the eyes of his family, only friend, those in authoritative positions and even strangers. As his sins weigh on his soul and drag him further and further to the ground the truth all but comes out. He confesses eventually and is imprisoned.

    Some of the characters like Svidrigailov are really interesting — some sort of foil to Raskolnikov. Porfiry just sees straight through Rodya. He must be some sort of reflection of modern man. From his fat face to his indulgent personality he is familiar in all ways and is, frustratingly, right in the end.

    Rodya is somewhat a tragic character. Despite his mood swings and bouts of insanity he did truly care for his mother and sister and in my opinion Sonya to some degree. She was the only person meek and kind enough to whom he could fully confess and confide in.

    Notes from Underground (1864)

    I remember just how much I looked up to the Underground Man the first time I read this. I'm not sure if I wanted to be him, understand him, or just know if what he was saying was real or if he was just a raving lunatic. I do know that the fact that he existed on both sides of this line amazed me. Another thing that amazed me was just how much I related to some parts of his writing. It startled me, even when I read it this time, and honestly embarrassed me that I found so many qualities of myself in this fucking degenerate man. This forty-year-old worthless bastard who flails about when trying to be a human being.

    I've come to believe that the underground is not necessarily a place as much as it is a mode of being. I'd say you're born into it — it's how you're wired. I'd also say there's no escaping it. The underground man has only been underground for as long as he's been able to know he was underground, and after that point there was never any chance of returning. It's a state of vigilance and tumultuous emotion. You can feel things but they aren't morally aligned with what is right and wrong, though one is altogether too aware of what is right and wrong.

    This book was weird. I laughed at times, and was disgusted a lot more times. I will have to keep returning to it.
