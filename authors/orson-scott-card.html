    Orson Scott Card

    Speaker for the Dead (1986)

    Insane. Favorite book of the entire series. Having finished the original 4 and coming back to write this review, I honestly should have just stopped here. Personally, this is where I was the most absolutely invested in the novel, the characters, the plot — everything. So many mind-blowing concepts were introduced at the time and it was addicting to read. Will genuinely skip all other parts and just reread this one section of the series. After rereading I'll decide whether or not to ditch the copies of the other books I own for this one.

    Ender's Game (1985)

    This book put me back on my passion for reading again and I'm grateful for it. It gave me the antsy anticipation of a story very much unfolding between my eyes, and I can remember reading it every chance I got ever since finding that old, busted copy in the box at home. As soon as I finished it for the first time I quickly went to Barnes and Nobles to purchase the whole series. Anyway, the ending is what really took me for a turn and made me start thinking more creatively and introduced a sense of criticism to everything else I've been reading.

    Xenocide (1991)

    Even after the ridiculous amount of time I spent getting through the book, Card's stories have a consistency and utter cohesiveness that I could never hope to emulate. I'm starting to understand that a major part of this seeming cohesiveness is the simple structure of the novels. Much like any anime I've watched or movie series, there isn't just one story. This complexity makes the story organic in a way, mainly for its unpredictable, almost erratic nature, especially at the end of the book.

    Again, the writing in this book, much like the first two, was amazing. I think my favorite parts may be the dialogue between human and the hive queen in the beginning of each chapter as they attempt to analyze humans, each other and themselves — lots of really good quotes and interesting takes in there.

    The ending completely changes the future of the book forever. Not only does the invention of faster-than-lightspeed travel now completely rest on the metaphorical shoulders of Jane, but the method of it allows individuals to create anything, even other life forms out of nothing. A good book overall — ranking this one highly as a factor of its creativity and intense scenes.

    Children of the Mind (1996)

    I have mixed feelings about this book. As always the prose, language and storytelling ability of Card do not fail, but when it comes to the actual story I'm left somewhere uncertain. I think the main part of this book was understanding the behavior and implications of the aiuas. Those scenes were all very interesting to read but I'm a bit disappointed since I didn't get to find out what exactly was up with the descoladas — I'll just look it up at this point.
