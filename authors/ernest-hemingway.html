    Ernest Hemingway

    The Sun Also Rises (1926)

    Unlike any kind of romance or drama or historical fiction I've read so far. Hemingway popularized a kind of short, direct prose that was just as effective at being blunt and direct as it was eloquent. I'm not sure what to make of it still, to be honest.

    The characters that stand out the most to me are Brett and Jake, maybe Michael too. Brett, gorgeous and alluring, lives off others, picking her interests on instinct alone it seems like. She calls herself a bitch endlessly — I think because she thinks that she isn't really built for love, or at the very least hasn't found a partner with which to build any kind of stable relationship. If anything, the most stable relationship she has is with the protagonist Jake.

    At first it really seems like they love each other, but I'm not sure exactly how Brett loves others if at all. She's attracted, very much infatuated with her partners, but as they become people and leave her realm of fantasy and chance, she seems to remove herself from them before there's too much damage. For their sake more so than hers, which is admirable to a point. The only problem I have is that a problem has been identified but it seems like she's more than okay with just living with it, then expects those around her to do the same.

    Maybe Jake's love is truly in attention — but despite knowing he loves her he resigns himself to this caretaker role. On the other hand, Brett doesn't want to stay with him, either because she can't live the kind of frivolous lifestyle she desires, or something deeper.
